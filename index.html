Glossary

Below are definitions for some of the scientific jargon used in our articles.

Protostar
A collapsing region of a molecular cloud marked by the early stages of star formation before nuclear fusion ignites in its core.
Star Cluster
A group of stars that share a common origin and are held together by gravity, often formed from the same molecular cloud.
Main Sequence
The phase in a star's life during which it is converting hydrogen into helium in its core, representing the majority of a star's "life."
Stellar Nursery
A region within a molecular cloud where star formation is actively occurring.
Accretion Disk
A rotating disk of matter, often formed by material falling onto a central object, such as a protostar, during the process of star formation.
Protoplanetary/Circumstellar Disk

T Tauri Star
A transitional stage between infrared stars surrounded by opaque nebulae and stable stars that have settled on the main sequence.
Supernova
A powerful explosion of a star.
White Dwarf
The stage after nuclear fuel exhaustion for low and medium-mass stars.
Galactic Halor
The spherical component of a galaxy that surrounds the galactic disk, containing older stars and globular clusters.
Pulsar
A highly magnetized, rotating neutron star that emits beams of electromagnetic radiation out of its magnetic poles
Dark Matter
Hypothetical matter that does not emit, absorb, or reflect light, and is believed to make up approximately 85% of the matter in the universe, influencing the large-scale structure of the cosmos
Gamma-Ray Burst (GRB)
An extremely energetic astronomical event that releases a burst of gamma-ray photons, often associated with the collapse of massive stars or the collision of compact objects
Quasar
A highly energetic and distant active galactic nucleus, powered by a supermassive black hole at the center of a galaxy
Stellar wind
A flow of charged particles (plasma) ejected from a star, often during its formation or later stages of evolution. Stellar winds play a role in shaping the surrounding interstellar medium.
Molecular cloud
A cold and dense interstellar cloud composed mostly of molecular hydrogen. These clouds are the primary sites for the formation of stars and planetary systems.
Nuclear Fusion
The process in which atomic nuclei combine to form a more massive nucleus, releasing energy; it is the fundamental mechanism powering stars.
Gravitational Collapse
The process in which a region of higher-than-average density undergoes self-amplifying gravitational forces, leading to star formation.
Cosmic Dust
Tiny solid particles, often composed of silicates and carbon compounds, that exist in the interstellar medium and play a crucial role in star and planet formation.
ISM chemistry
ISM stands for Interstellar Medium, which refers to the matter and energy that exists between stars in a galaxy.
the Pleiades
an extremely hot cluster of young stars. Also known as the 'Seven Sisters' or M45, this stellar family has been the subject of numerous myths in cultures worldwide
Hyades
the Hyades cluster is the closest star cluster to Earth, at a distance of 150 light-years. This cluster is very easy to spot in the night sky, because it has a compact and distinctive shape of the letter V
